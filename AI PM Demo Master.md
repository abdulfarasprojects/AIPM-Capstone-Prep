# AIPM Demo Master Guide

This guide is based on the notes, summaries, and transcript patterns from cohort 6 and cohort 7 demo days.

## 1. What the judges seem to score

In cohort 7, the judging rubric was stated explicitly in three parts:

1. **Problem and discovery**
2. **AI solution and evaluation**
3. **Storytelling and delivery**

The raw Q&A across both cohorts suggests one more practical truth: the judges use questions to find the weakest part of the presentation. If something is vague, they go there. If something is impressive but risky, they go there too.

## 2. What strong demos had in common

Across the winners and runners-up, the strongest demos consistently had these traits:

- A very clear user and a very clear pain point
- A focused wedge instead of a broad platform pitch
- A workflow that made the AI feel integrated, not bolted on
- A believable end-to-end demo, even if the product was still early
- Evidence of testing, evaluation, or at least a credible plan for evaluation
- Direct answers during Q&A
- A polished pace and simple storytelling

What usually triggered tougher questions:

- Broad or fuzzy ICP
- Too much manual workflow friction
- Unclear reason this needed AI
- Weak explanation of what is better than a generic LLM
- No proof that the problem is real
- No plan for failure modes, trust, or safety
- Weak GTM or monetization logic

## 3. Recommended structure for the four-minute video

Treat the video as an executive product review, not a feature tour.

### 0:00-0:25 - Hook and user pain

Open with one specific user and one specific pain.

What to say:

- Who the user is
- What painful moment they face
- Why it matters now

Good formula:

`[User] is trying to [job], but today they have to [current workaround], which leads to [time loss / risk / bad experience / missed revenue].`

### 0:25-0:50 - Discovery and why this problem matters

Very quickly establish that this is a real problem, not just an idea.

Include:

- How you validated it
- What repeated signal you heard
- What surprised you

If you have no user interviews, use the strongest evidence you do have, but be honest.

### 0:50-1:10 - Product one-liner and wedge

State the product in one sentence.

Formula:

`We built [product name], an AI-powered [product category] for [ICP] that helps them [core outcome] by [main mechanism].`

Then state the wedge:

- What is the one feature or workflow entry point you are starting with?
- Why did you start there?

### 1:10-2:35 - Demo the value moment

This is the center of the presentation.

The demo should show:

1. The input or starting context
2. What the AI is doing
3. What output the user gets
4. What action the user takes next
5. Why this is better than today

Important:

- Show the key value moment early
- Keep the path narrow
- Do not click through too many screens
- Do not narrate every UI element
- Make the workflow look believable in the real world

The judges repeatedly care about workflow fit, so make sure they can answer this question by the end of the demo: `Where does this fit in the user's actual day-to-day process?`

### 2:35-3:00 - Why AI, specifically

You need a crisp answer for why this is an AI product and not a normal software workflow.

Cover:

- What job AI is doing
- Why rules/manual search/templates are not enough
- Why a generic prompt in ChatGPT is weaker than your product

Good structure:

- `AI handles [reasoning / generation / retrieval / classification / personalization]`
- `Deterministic systems handle [routing / guardrails / storage / actions / integrations]`
- `This is better than a generic LLM because [context, workflow, evaluation, structured outputs, proprietary data, or actionability]`

### 3:00-3:25 - How it works and how you know it works

Do not over-explain the architecture, but do show technical credibility.

Cover only the essentials:

- What data or inputs the system uses
- Basic architecture if relevant
- How you evaluated quality
- How you handle low-confidence or failure cases

This is where many cohort questions came from.

### 3:25-3:45 - Business logic

Include just enough business reality.

Answer:

- Who pays?
- Why will they pay?
- What is the initial market?
- How do you get first users or pilots?

Keep it brief. Judges do ask GTM questions even when the demo is strong.

### 3:45-4:00 - Close

End with a clear summary:

- The problem
- The product wedge
- The core value delivered
- The next step

Do not end on a technical screen. End on the user value or the outcome.

## 4. What should be included in your script

Your script should include all of the following, even if each item is only one sentence.

### Must-have content

- The exact user or ICP
- The exact pain point
- The current workaround
- Why this matters now
- Your product one-liner
- Your wedge feature
- Where the product fits in workflow
- What the AI is doing
- What is deterministic vs AI-driven
- What proof you have that it works
- What happens when it fails or is uncertain
- Why this is better than a generic LLM
- Why users will adopt it
- Why someone would pay for it

### Four-minute script skeleton

Use this as a fill-in template.

`Hi, I'm [name], and this is [product].`

`[User] is trying to [job], but today they have to [current workaround]. That leads to [cost, frustration, risk, or missed opportunity].`

`Through [interviews / research / pilots / domain experience], I saw that the repeated pain was [top repeated pain].`

`So I built [product], an AI-powered [category] for [ICP] that helps them [outcome].`

`I am starting with [wedge feature] because it is the fastest path to value and the most painful part of the workflow.`

`Let me show you how it works.`

`The user starts by [input or trigger]. From there, the system uses [data/context] to [AI task].`

`What the user gets is [output], which helps them [decision/action/outcome].`

`The key difference versus a generic LLM is [workflow context / structured output / integrated action / proprietary data / evaluation / safety].`

`Under the hood, AI handles [reasoning/generation/classification], while the product handles [guardrails/integrations/storage/routing].`

`To evaluate it, I used [eval method], and I focused on [accuracy/relevance/safety/actionability/etc.].`

`If the system is uncertain or wrong, [fallback, human review, confidence gating, or escalation].`

`We would start with [initial market or buyer], and the business model is [pricing/monetization logic].`

`In short, [product] helps [ICP] go from [painful before-state] to [valuable after-state]. Thank you.`

## 5. Recording guidance for the video

The judges commented on pacing and clarity, so the recording quality matters more than people think.

### Recording rules

- Aim for roughly 500 to 600 spoken words total
- Speak slower than you think you need to
- Use short sentences
- Show value by the first minute
- Keep one idea per slide or screen
- Use zoom generously so text is readable
- Hide notifications, bookmarks clutter, and unnecessary tabs
- Use a clean browser profile or a cleaned-up desktop
- Keep your cursor movement slow and intentional

### Screen flow

Use this order if possible:

1. Opening slide or opening visual with problem statement
2. One slide on user/problem/discovery
3. Product demo
4. One simple slide on why AI + evaluation
5. Closing slide with value and next step

### Audio and delivery

- Record in a quiet room
- Use headphones or a decent microphone
- Do one short audio test first
- Re-record if your energy drops halfway through
- Avoid sounding like you are reading a deck
- Sound like you are explaining a product to execs

### Practical prep for recordings

- Prepare a full script
- Then mark breath points and emphasis words
- Rehearse until you can say it without sounding scripted
- Record at least two versions
- Watch once muted to check clarity of visuals
- Watch once without looking at the screen to check clarity of narration

## 6. How to prepare for live judge questions

The most common question themes across cohorts were:

1. ICP and market focus
2. Problem validation and discovery
3. Why AI and why not just ChatGPT
4. Product wedge and MVP scope
5. Workflow fit and onboarding
6. Data sources and technical feasibility
7. Evaluation and real-world quality
8. Failure modes, safety, privacy, and trust
9. Differentiation and moat
10. Go-to-market and acquisition
11. Monetization and ROI
12. Roadmap and what comes next

### Best structure for answering questions

Use this answer structure:

`Direct answer -> evidence -> tradeoff -> next step`

Example:

`We are starting with small independent clinics because the admin pain is acute there and buying decisions are faster. We saw that pattern in [interviews/research]. The tradeoff is that this narrows the initial market, but it gives us a clearer workflow and faster validation. The next step would be running pilots with [target segment].`

This structure works because it keeps you direct, concrete, and thoughtful.

### How to handle a question you are weak on

Do not bluff.

Instead say:

- `That is the main risk I still need to validate.`
- `My current hypothesis is [x].`
- `What I would test next is [y].`

That sounds much stronger than a vague answer.

## 7. How to get more information on common judge questions

You already have the right raw material in this folder: summaries, question pattern notes, and full transcripts.

The best way to turn that into reusable prep is this:

### Step 1 - Build a question bank

For every cohort file, collect every judge question into one list.

Tag each question by one of these themes:

- User / ICP
- Problem / pain
- Validation
- Why AI
- Workflow fit
- Wedge / MVP
- Technical design
- Evals
- Safety / reliability
- Differentiation
- GTM
- Monetization
- Roadmap

### Step 2 - Count the repeats

Once tagged, count which themes show up most often.

Across these cohorts, the repeat themes were especially:

- Workflow fit
- Clear ICP
- Why AI vs generic tools
- Validation of the problem
- Evaluation quality
- Trust / safety / reliability
- GTM realism

### Step 3 - Turn each theme into a prep card

For each theme, prepare four things:

1. A 15-second answer
2. A 45-second deeper answer
3. One piece of proof or evidence
4. One honest limitation plus next step

That makes it much easier to answer variations of the same judge question.

### Step 4 - Prepare a source-of-truth sheet

Before rehearsing Q&A, prepare one page of facts for your project:

- Who is the user?
- Who is the buyer?
- What pain did you validate?
- What evidence supports it?
- What is the wedge?
- Why AI?
- Why not a generic LLM?
- What data does the system use?
- How do you evaluate outputs?
- What happens when it fails?
- How do you get users?
- Why do they pay?

If this sheet exists, answering questions becomes much easier because you are pulling from prepared facts, not improvising from scratch.

### Step 5 - Do weak-link mock Q&A

Ask a friend, classmate, or mentor to do this specifically:

- Listen to the demo once
- Identify the weakest link
- Ask only tough questions about that area

This mirrors how the judges behave.

## 8. What to have ready before demo day

Have these ready in writing before you record or rehearse live Q&A:

1. One-line problem statement
2. One-line ICP statement
3. Three discovery facts
4. One-line product wedge
5. One-line why-AI statement
6. One-line why-not-ChatGPT statement
7. Two evaluation metrics
8. Two failure modes and fallbacks
9. One GTM entry strategy
10. One pricing or monetization logic

If you can answer those ten items quickly and directly, you will be far better prepared than most presenters.

## 9. Fast rehearsal plan

### Round 1 - Structure check

Ask: does the story clearly answer problem, product, AI value, proof, and business logic?

### Round 2 - Time check

Ask: am I showing value fast enough, or am I still setting up the problem too late?

### Round 3 - Weak-link Q&A

Ask: if a judge wanted to stress-test the weakest part of this pitch, what would they ask?

### Round 4 - Final polish

Ask: does this sound executive-level, concise, and confident?

## 10. Final reminder

The goal is not to sound like you built the most technically complex system in the cohort.

The goal is to make the judges believe all of the following at once:

- This is a real problem
- For a real user
- Solved in a focused way
- With AI used for the right reason
- In a workflow that makes sense
- With evidence that it can work
- Presented clearly and credibly

If you do that, your odds of handling both the video and the Q&A improve a lot.
